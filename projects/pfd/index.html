Probability-Flow Distillation: Exact Wasserstein Gradient Flow for High-Fidelity 3D Generation

Rohith Ramanan1, A. N. Rajagopalan1
1Indian Institute of Technology Madras
arXiv Code (coming soon)

Abstract

TL;DR

SDS-style methods enable sampling in parameter space via optimization but suffer from mode collapse and biased gradients; we propose Probability-Flow Distillation (PFD), inducing Wasserstein gradient flow for improved distribution matching.

Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) and its variants have been widely used for text-to-3D generation by distilling 2D image diffusion priors. However, the standard SDS objective is prone to severe mode collapse, frequently yielding over-smoothed and over-saturated results. Although recent advancements, such as Score Distillation via Inversion (SDI), mitigate these artifacts and produce visually sharper models, they ultimately fail to faithfully capture the full target distribution.

In this work, we show that the bottleneck limiting the sampling capacity of SDI stems from its reliance on the posterior mean estimator, which is mathematically equivalent to a single-step Euler approximation of the deterministic reverse DDIM trajectory. To address this, we propose Probability-Flow Distillation (PFD), replacing this approximation with exact integration of the PF-ODE. We establish that PFD corresponds exactly to a Wasserstein gradient flow, inducing principled distribution-matching dynamics. PFD synthesizes 3D assets with fine-grained, high-fidelity details and achieves improved quality compared to existing methods.

Key Observation

The central observation motivating PFD concerns the DDIM posterior mean estimator. Given a noisy observation xt, the standard estimate of the clean sample is:

𝔼[x0 | xt] = x̃t − σt εφ(xt, t)

We show this is mathematically equivalent to a single explicit forward Euler discretization step of the DDIM Probability-Flow ODE, evaluated at (xt, t) and integrating along the reverse-time trajectory from t to 0 with step size Δσ = −σt. In other words, the posterior mean is not an exact denoiser — it is a first-order numerical approximation of a continuous ODE, with all the truncation error that entails.

SDS builds its gradient on this estimate, and SDI improves the forward pass with DDIM inversion but still uses this single-step approximation in the reverse pass. Both are therefore fundamentally limited by this coarse discretization. The natural fix is to replace the single Euler step with exact numerical integration of the PF-ODE in both directions — which is precisely what PFD does.

Figure 1. (a) SDS: noisy sample via Gaussian perturbation; reverse via single Euler step (posterior mean). (b) SDI: forward via DDIM inversion; reverse still a single Euler step. (c) PFD (Ours): both forward and reverse via exact PF-ODE integration, eliminating the posterior mean approximation entirely.

Probability-Flow Distillation

Beyond fixing the numerical approximation, PFD has a deeper theoretical character. We prove that the expected PFD gradient estimator exactly matches the Wasserstein gradient of a time-averaged KL divergence functional between the generated distribution and the target diffusion prior:

𝔼t[Δt] = ∇x δℱ[q0] / δq0(x)

where ℱ[q0] = 𝔼t[w(t) DKL(qt ∥ pt)]. This means the particle dynamics induced by PFD constitute a gradient flow in Wasserstein space, with a global minimum at q0 = p0 — the target distribution. PFD is therefore not merely a heuristic improvement over SDS; it is a principled distribution-matching algorithm.

Intuitively, SDS is mode-seeking (it drives particles toward a single high-probability point), whereas PFD is distribution-matching (it drives the entire particle ensemble toward the target distribution). The 2D experiment below makes this concrete.

(b) SDI partially recovers the support but misses fine structure. (c) PFD (Ours) faithfully reconstructs the full multi-modal distribution.

Text-to-3D Generation

We apply PFD to text-to-3D generation as a natural testbed for the algorithm. It is important to emphasize: text-to-3D is not the primary contribution of this work. The core contribution is the PFD algorithm itself — a general procedure for optimizing parameters of any differentiable representation by inducing Wasserstein gradient flow. Text-to-3D is simply a compelling and natural domain to demonstrate the power of the method.

To make the method tractable, we employ approximations akin to those used implicitly in prior work. Rather than maintaining a full particle ensemble and learning the score of the current particle distribution (as the exact algorithm requires), we use standard DDIM inversion as a surrogate for the forward PF-ODE and operate in the single-particle regime (N = 1). These approximations substantially cut computational cost while preserving the key distributional benefits of PFD.

Despite this crude instantiation, PFD achieves results competitive with VSD — the current state-of-the-art distribution-matching method, which requires LoRA fine-tuning of the diffusion model and ~45 GB VRAM — while maintaining memory usage comparable to SDI (~36 GB). PFD also substantially outperforms SDI in texture fidelity and perceptual quality, particularly at higher resolutions where the gap becomes pronounced.

A note to the community

The text-to-3D implementation presented here is deliberately minimal — many hyperparameters and design choices (ODE solver order, timestep scheduling, representation architecture, guidance strategies, multi-view consistency) are largely untouched. We strongly encourage the community to build on PFD: better instantiations are likely to yield substantially improved results. See the paper for a detailed discussion of future directions, including more efficient PF-ODE solvers, trajectory distillation, and extensions beyond the single-particle regime.
